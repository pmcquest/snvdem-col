We have two kinds of variables: the weights, which are derived from the V-Dem expert ratings; and the scores, which are variables measured at the municipal level using data from DANE, election returns, etc. We have different weights by dimension, d, that we're measuring (urban-rural, distance from Bogota, etc.); and weights change over years, t, also. So the weights can be written weight_dt (underscore here denoting a subscript). 

The scores are different for each dimension, d, municipio, i, and for each year, t. So the scores term is score_dit. 

The index formula for emel is therefore sum from 1 to d of (weight_dt * score_dit).

We've decided that the weights for emel and cscw will be combined within years by subtracting less from more (or vice versa), and weak from strong, and by taking the absolute value. This ensures that the (net) weight is positive, and therefore reflects the magnitude of the contribution of the score to the index, not the direction of the contribution (more democracy or less). To ensure that the index is calculated correctly, we have to make sure that score_it for each dimension is calculated so that the high values are associated with greater democracy and the low values are associated with less democracy. In many cases, that is straightforward, because we expect high values of per capita GDP, for example, are associated with more democracy. That is true for urban % of population, too. However, the rurality index is the reverse. So before rurality is combined with urbanization (I can't remember whether we're combining two variables here or just using rurality, but for this example, let's say we are combining those two), rurality has to be flipped so that rural areas have low values and urban areas have high values. Assuming this is a CDF already, that can be done by subtracting rurality from 1: 1-rurality. Then you could average urban pop and 1-rurality to get something meaningful to multiply by the urban-rural weight. 

Same for all the other dimensions: make sure high values go with expected higher democracy and low values go with expected lower democracy.
